Mini “exploratory report” on juvenile snowshoe hares in Bonanza Creek Experimental Forest

Introduction

This report explores the counts and sizes (weight and hind foot length) of juvenile snowshoe hares recorded from 1998 - 2012 as part of the Bonanza Creek LTER (Long Term Ecological Research site). While there are many possible future analyses with regards to these data, this report focuses on the juvenile aged hares and the overall trap counts during the study period, the weight distributions between sexes and across sites, and the relationship between weight and hind foot length. Snowshoe hares are keystone prey species and their population densities, as well as population health are important, specifically at landscape levels, to understand.

Data and Analysis Methods

Snowshoe hare physical data was collected by Kielland, Knut et al. in the Bonanza Creek Experimental Forest, a Long Term Ecological Research (LTER) site in Alaska. These data were collected from 1998-present. While the dataset includes 3,197 observations, for the purpose of this report, we will only be looking at juvenile aged snowshoe hares. In this report when using two-sample t-tests a significance level of 0.05 is understood. The effect size between groups is evaluated with a Cohen’s d test. Finally, linear models are included when exploring the relationship between weight and hind foot length of juvenile snowshoe hares with simple linear regression and Pearson’s r. All analyses are in R version 4.0.2 using RStudio version 1.3.1073.

The maximum count of juvenile snowshoe hares was 126 and was recorded in 1999 whereas the minimum count of 2 was recorded in 2010. The mean number of juvenile hares trapped for the years 1998 - 2012 was 31.5 and the median was 18.5. There is a large variability in trap counts from year to year. Generally, over the time period this study was carried out, the count of juvenile hares trapped is decreasing. The maximum count may be reflective of a higher degree of effort (time and trap count) during the 1999 season. Conversely, the years of low trap counts may indicate limited time or energy placed on the study, or lower trap numbers in those years. Moving forward, it would be a more robust study to include the time spent during each year as well as the number of traps included in the study each year to better standardize the data. As may be expected with mammal species, the male juvenile snowshoe hares of this study were, on average (mean weight), heavier than the female juvenile hares (945.86 \(\pm\) 333.22 g, n = 163 and 855.39 \(\pm\) 292.25 g, n = 197, respectively; mean \(\pm\) 1 standard deviation). The absolute difference in means of weights between the groups is 90.47g which is a percent difference of 2.51%. The outcome of a Welch Two-Sample t-test indicates the difference of means is significant (t(325.02) = -2.71, p = 0.007 and the effect size is small (Cohen’s d = -0.29). It is important to examine the effect size in addition to significance testing because it is not influenced by sample size. Although the p-value is less than 0.05 and therefore is considered to be significant, the effect size is small and therefore the mean weights of the female and male juvenile hares are not actually very different from one another.

The linear model is best fit by the method of ordinary least squares. Despite my boss’s inclination to try a linear regression to describe the relationship between juvenile snowshoe hare weight and hind foot length, it may not be the best explanation for the data. While there is an overall weak/medium positive correlation between hind foot length and body weight, the data points are not all close to the linear regression line. The slope of the linear model (9.52) indicates that we expect the weight of a juvenile snowshoe hare to increase by 9.52 grams for every 1 mm increase of hind foot length. The Coefficient of Determination (R2) is 0.299,signifying that 29.9% of variance of the dependent variable (weight (g)) can be predicted by the value of the independent variable (hind foot length (mm)). This is not a very high correlation and therefore other explanations of the data may need to be looked at.
When we look at the Pearson’s r value of correlation, it is calculated to be 0.55 which indicates a medium positive correlation between the two variables.

The assumption of homoscedascity, or constant variance, of the errors is of concern with these data. The residuals are uneven across the range of the model. This unevenness has the potential to error in estimates for predictions. This model exhibits heteroscedastity in the spread of residuals. Summary and Initial Findings

  • The trap counts of juvenile snowshoe hares decreased over the course of the study period (1998-2012).
  • There were fewer total juvenile snowshoe hares counted in the Black Spruce areas than the mature forest and riparian zone.
  • In all three sites of Bonanza Creek Experimental Forest the male juvenile hares weighed more on average (higher central tendencies) than the female juvenile hares, while this was more apparent in the mature and riparian areas. It is important to note that while the outcome of the two-sample t-test indicated a very small percent (.7%) chance that samples taken from populations with the same mean would have means at least as different as seen here if taken randomly, the actual percent difference between means of male and female juvenile hares was only 2.51% and there was a small effect size (Cohen’s d).
  • There is a medium positive correlation between juvenile hare weight and hind foot length, where a 29.9% of variance of weight may be predicted by the hind foot length value.
